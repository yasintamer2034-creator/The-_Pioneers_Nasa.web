Meet Cora — a curious solar storm

Far away on the Sun, a bright burst of energy pops free — that's Cora, a coronal mass ejection (CME). She is a huge bubble of charged gas and magnetic field hurtling outward from the Sun.

As she travels, she may cause beautiful auroras, but she can also jiggle radios, disturb GPS, stress power grids, and sometimes affect satellites and astronauts.

What NASA Teaches Us About Space Weather

Space weather is the changing set of conditions in space caused by the Sun’s activity — not light or heat, but charged particles, magnetic fields, and radiation.

Here are some important things NASA and other scientists have learned:

  • Solar flares are intense bursts of radiation (X-rays and ultraviolet) that travel at light speed: their effects reach Earth in about 8 minutes.
  • Coronal Mass Ejections (CMEs) are slower but massive clouds of plasma and embedded magnetic field that may take hours to days to reach Earth.
  • When a CME is fast enough, it can drive a shock wave ahead of it. This shock accelerates charged particles (protons, electrons) toward Earth.
  • Earth is protected by its magnetic field and atmosphere, which block most dangerous particles and radiation.
  • However, during strong events, the protective shield can be stressed. A “geomagnetic storm” results when Cora’s magnetic field interacts with Earth’s magnetosphere. This causes electrical currents in Earth’s upper atmosphere and even currents on the ground.
  • These currents can overload power grids, stress transformers, and cause voltage fluctuations.
  • Satellites in orbit face danger from energetic particles (which can damage electronics, cause bit flips, or degrade sensors) and from changing drag in Earth's upper atmosphere.
  • In extreme historical storms, such as the March 1989 event, major power outages occurred.
  • In May 2024, NASA observed dozens of strong solar flares including some X-class ones, demonstrating that the Sun is active and space weather is very real.
  • Scientists are building improved forecasting tools, such as AI models and new satellites, to better warn us in advance.
